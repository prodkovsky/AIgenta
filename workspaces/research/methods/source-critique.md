# Source Critique Method

## Overview

Source critique is the systematic evaluation of a source's credibility, reliability, and relevance. In AIgenta's research workspace, this method helps you assess whether sources are trustworthy and appropriate for your purposes.

## Purpose

Use the source critique method when you need to:

- Evaluate the quality of individual sources
- Decide which sources to include or exclude from your work
- Assess the strength of evidence supporting claims
- Identify potential biases or limitations in sources
- Establish credibility in your work by using high-quality sources
- Provide justification for your source selections

## When to Use This Method

**Appropriate contexts:**
- During literature review to evaluate potential sources
- When sources make controversial or surprising claims
- When assessing conflicting information from different sources
- When writing for academic or professional audiences
- When building arguments that depend on source quality

**Not appropriate when:**
- Using widely accepted facts (e.g., historical dates, scientific constants)
- Incorporating your own original ideas or experiences
- Creating creative or opinion-based content
- When source quality is clearly established and uncontroversial

## Critique Dimensions

### 1. Authority and Expertise

**Questions to ask:**
- Who is the author? What are their credentials?
- What organization published this? What's its reputation?
- Does the author have expertise in this specific area?
- Has this author written other credible works on this topic?

**Evidence of good authority:**
- Author has advanced degrees or professional experience in the field
- Publisher is a recognized academic institution, reputable journal, or established industry source
- Author has a track record of credible publications
- Source includes author affiliations and credentials

**Red flags:**
- No author listed or author has no evident expertise
- Publisher is unknown, partisan, or has questionable reputation
- Author is writing outside their area of expertise
- Source lacks publication information

### 2. Currency and Timeliness

**Questions to ask:**
- When was this published?
- Is the information still current?
- Have there been significant developments since publication?
- Does this field change rapidly, making currency crucial?

**Evidence of good currency:**
- Recent publication date (within last 3-5 years for rapidly changing fields)
- Source acknowledges recent developments
- Information aligns with current understanding
- Author updates or revises as needed

**Red flags:**
- Publication date is significantly old for a rapidly changing field
- Source is outdated by major events or discoveries
- Author doesn't acknowledge newer research or developments
- Information contradicts current understanding without explanation

### 3. Purpose and Bias

**Questions to ask:**
- What is the purpose of this source? To inform, persuade, sell, entertain?
- Is there an agenda or perspective that shapes the content?
- Does the source acknowledge its perspective or bias?
- Is the language objective or loaded/emotional?

**Evidence of good purpose:**
- Clear purpose stated or evident
- Balanced presentation of multiple perspectives
- Objective, neutral language
- Acknowledges limitations and alternative views
- Distinguishes between fact and opinion

**Red flags:**
- Hidden agenda or unstated purpose
- One-sided presentation without acknowledging other views
- Emotionally loaded or persuasive language masquerading as fact
- Advertising or promotional material disguised as information
- Lack of transparency about perspective or funding

### 4. Accuracy and Evidence

**Questions to ask:**
- What evidence supports claims? Data, research, examples?
- Are claims specific and precise?
- Can claims be verified from other sources?
- Are there logical fallacies or unsupported assertions?

**Evidence of good accuracy:**
- Claims are supported by specific evidence (data, studies, examples)
- Sources are cited for factual claims
- Information aligns with what you know from other credible sources
- Author acknowledges uncertainty or limitations
- Claims are qualified appropriately

**Red flags:**
- General or vague claims without support
- No citations or evidence provided
- Claims contradict well-established knowledge without explanation
- Logical fallacies (appeals to emotion, straw man, etc.)
- Overconfident assertions without qualification

### 5. Audience and Scope

**Questions to ask:**
- Who is the intended audience? Experts, general public, students?
- Is the depth appropriate for your purposes?
- Does it cover the scope you need?
- Is the scope appropriate given the length/depth?

**Evidence of good audience/scope match:**
- Technical level matches your needs and audience
- Covers the aspects of the topic relevant to your work
- Depth is appropriate (not too superficial or too specialized)
- Scope aligns with stated purpose

**Red flags:**
- Target audience is very different from yours
- Misses key aspects of the topic you need
- Too superficial for serious use
- Too narrow or specialized for your purposes

### 6. Relevance and Utility

**Questions to ask:**
- How directly does this address your research question?
- What unique value does it provide?
- Does it offer something other sources don't?
- How will you actually use this in your work?

**Evidence of good relevance:**
- Directly addresses your research question or need
- Provides unique information or perspective
- Offers concrete examples, data, or frameworks you can use
- Clearly informs your project

**Red flags:**
- Only tangentially related to your topic
- Offers nothing unique compared to other sources
- Too general or high-level to be useful
- Doesn't provide actionable insights or information

## Critique Workflow

### Step 1: Identify Key Information
- Record full citation (author, title, publication, date, URL)
- Note author credentials and publisher
- Identify publication date and context
- Scan for purpose and audience

### Step 2: Evaluate Each Dimension
- Assess authority, currency, purpose, accuracy, scope, relevance
- Note strengths and weaknesses
- Identify any red flags
- Rate overall quality (High, Medium, Low)

### Step 3: Make a Decision
- Include or exclude from your work
- If included, note how you'll use it and any limitations
- If excluded, document why (for transparency)

### Step 4: Document Your Assessment
- Keep a record of your critique
- Note key points and evidence
- Record how you used (or didn't use) the source

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### ❌ Appeal to Authority
**Problem**: Assuming a source is credible because the author is famous or the publisher is prestigious
**Solution**: Evaluate each source on its own merits; even credible sources can be wrong or biased

### ❌ Dismissal Based on Bias
**Problem**: Rejecting a source entirely because it has a perspective or bias
**Solution**: Acknowledge bias, but assess what valuable information the source still provides

### ❌ Over-Skepticism
**Problem**: Rejecting sources because you can't verify every single claim
**Solution**: Focus on overall credibility and evidence; some uncertainty is inevitable

### ❌ Confirmation Bias
**Problem**: Accepting sources that agree with your view and rejecting those that don't
**Solution**: Evaluate all sources using the same criteria; seek out diverse perspectives

### ❌ Surface-Level Assessment
**Problem**: Only checking credentials or publication date without deeper analysis
**Solution**: Examine content for accuracy, evidence, and logical coherence

## Quality Checklist

A credible source typically:

- [ ] Has identifiable author with relevant expertise
- [ ] Published by reputable organization or outlet
- [ ] Recent enough to be current for the field
- [ ] States purpose and audience clearly
- [ ] Supports claims with evidence
- [ ] Acknowledges limitations and alternative views
- [ ] Distinguishes between fact and opinion
- [ ] Aligns with other credible sources
- [ ] Is directly relevant to your needs
- [ ] Provides unique value

## Integration with Other Methods

- **Literature Review**: Use source critique to evaluate potential sources before inclusion
- **Synthesis**: Weight sources differently in synthesis based on quality
- **Data Analysis**: Assess the credibility of data sources and methodologies
