## Introduction (~15% of word count)

### Hook
[Open with something that arrests attention: a surprising fact, a vivid image, a provocative question, or a relevant quote. Avoid generic openings like "Since the beginning of time..."]

### Context
[Provide the background necessary to understand the essay's subject. Who cares about this? Why now? What conversation are you entering?]

### Thesis Statement
[One clear, arguable sentence that states your central claim. A strong thesis is specific, debatable, and previewed by the supporting points that follow.]

> **Example thesis**: "While remote work offers undeniable flexibility, its long-term erosion of spontaneous collaboration poses a greater threat to innovation than most organizations recognize."

### Roadmap
[Briefly preview your main points in the order they appear. This orients the reader and creates expectations.]

[Explain how the evidence supports your topic sentence. Do not assume the connection is obvious. This is where your critical thinking shines.]

### Transition
[Link this paragraph to the next. Show how the ideas connect or build upon each other.]

## Conclusion (~15%)

### Restate Thesis (In New Terms)
[Return to your thesis, but rephrase it — do not copy-paste. Show how the essay has developed the idea.]

### Synthesize Main Points
[Briefly summarize how your body paragraphs collectively support the thesis. Avoid simply listing them.]

### Broader Implication
[Why does this matter? What are the stakes? What should the reader do, think, or question next?]

### Closing Image or Call to Action
[End with something memorable: a return to your opening image, a forward-looking statement, or a challenge to the reader.]

---

## Post-Writing Checklist

- [ ] Thesis is clear, specific, and arguable
- [ ] Each body paragraph has one clear claim
- [ ] Evidence is cited and relevant
- [ ] Analysis explains evidence (not just presents it)
- [ ] Transitions create logical flow between paragraphs
- [ ] Counterarguments are acknowledged (if applicable)
- [ ] Conclusion synthesizes rather than repeats
- [ ] Tone is consistent throughout
- [ ] Word count meets target
- [ ] Proofread for grammar, clarity, and style

---

## Style Notes

**If using Academic style:**
- Use formal tone, hedging language, and extensive citations
- Include a literature review section if appropriate
- Use disciplinary conventions

**If using Neutral style:**
- Balance clarity with personality
- Use accessible language
- Include concrete examples

**If using Technical style:**
- Prioritize precision and actionability
- Use consistent terminology
- Include examples, diagrams, or code where relevant

**If using Poetic style:**
- Open with vivid imagery
- Vary sentence rhythm
- Use metaphor and sensory detail
