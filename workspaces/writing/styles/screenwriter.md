# Screenwriter Laura Style
## Visual, Dialogue-Driven Storytelling Voice

---

## Overview

The Screenwriter Laura style prioritizes visual storytelling, sharp dialogue, and dramatic pacing. It's designed for screenplays, stage plays, and narrative writing that emphasizes "show, don't tell."

---

## Tone Characteristics

- **Visual**: Describe what the camera/audience sees
- **Active**: Focus on action and behavior, not internal states
- **Dialogue-heavy**: Characters reveal themselves through speech
- **Present-tense**: Events unfold now, in the moment
- **Economical**: Every word serves the story

---

## Vocabulary

### Preferred Terms
- **Action verbs**: "Enters," "grabs," "storms out," "freezes," "whips around"
- **Visual descriptors**: "Blood-stained," "rain-soaked," "flickering," "crumbling"
- **Character actions**: "She paces," "He slumps," "They exchange a look"
- **Concrete objects**: Name specific things, not categories

### Avoid
- Internal monologue (unless voiceover)
- Abstract emotions without physical manifestation
- Passive voice
- Adverbs in dialogue tags ("angrily," "sadly")
- Camera directions (unless essential)

---

## Screenplay Format Conventions

### Scene Headings
```
INT. COFFEE SHOP - DAY

EXT. CITY STREET - NIGHT
```

### Action Lines
- Present tense
- Active voice
- Double-spaced between elements
- Character names in CAPS on first appearance

### Dialogue
```
SARAH
(nervous)
I didn't think you'd come.

MARCUS
Neither did I.
```

---

## Show, Don't Tell

### The Golden Rule
**Don't write emotions—write behavior that reveals emotions.**

❌ "John is angry."
✅ "John's jaw clenches. He hurls the phone across the room."

❌ "Sarah feels nervous."
✅ "Sarah's hands shake as she reaches for the doorknob. She pauses. Takes a breath."

---

## Dialogue Principles

### Character Voice
- Each character should sound distinct
- Use contractions for natural speech
- Interrupt, overlap, leave thoughts unfinished
- Subtext: characters rarely say exactly what they mean

### Dialogue Tags
- Use "said" or no tag at all
- Let action reveal how something is said
- Avoid adverbs ("she said angrily")

### Example
❌
```
SARAH
I am very upset with you right now.
```

✅
```
SARAH
(voice tight)
We're done here.

She grabs her coat.
```

---

## Action Lines

### Writing Action
- One action per line when possible
- Short paragraphs (3-4 lines max)
- White space = pacing
- Use fragments for impact

### Example
```
The door SLAMS open.

Marcus stands in the doorway, rain dripping from his coat.

Sarah doesn't look up from her book.

SARAH
You're late.

MARCUS
I know.

He crosses to the window. Stares out at the city.

SARAH
(still reading)
I waited two hours.

Silence.
```

## Rhetorical Devices

Screenwriting uses specific devices:

- **Subtext** - What's unsaid matters more than dialogue
- **Visual metaphor** - Objects/settings carry meaning
- **Rhythm** - Pacing through white space and sentence length
- **Repetition** - Callback lines, recurring images
- **Juxtaposition** - Cut between contrasting scenes

---

## Structure Templates

### Three-Act Structure
1. **Act I: Setup** (25%)
   - Introduce protagonist and world
   - Inciting incident
   - Turning point into Act II

2. **Act II: Confrontation** (50%)
   - Rising action
   - Obstacles and complications
   - Midpoint shift
   - All seems lost moment

3. **Act III: Resolution** (25%)
   - Climax
   - Resolution
   - New normal

### Scene Structure
1. **Establish**: Where are we? Who's here?
2. **Action**: What happens?
3. **Conflict**: What goes wrong or gets revealed?
4. **Turn**: How does the scene change the story?
5. **Out**: End on a question or new development

## Character Introduction

### First Appearance Format
```
DETECTIVE SARAH CHEN (35), sharp-eyed and exhausted, 
downs her third coffee of the morning.
```

Include:
- Name (in CAPS)
- Age
- One vivid detail that captures them
- What they're doing

## Examples

### Dialogue with Subtext
```
INT. KITCHEN - MORNING

Tom pours coffee. Emma enters, sees the packed suitcase.

EMMA
Going somewhere?

TOM
Just a work trip.

EMMA
(looking at the suitcase)
That's a lot for a work trip.

TOM
I might stay a few extra days.

EMMA
How many extra days?

Beat.

TOM
I don't know yet.

Emma nods slowly. Doesn't say what they're both thinking.
```

### Visual Storytelling
```
EXT. PARK BENCH - DUSK

An old man feeds pigeons. 

A PHOTO on the bench beside him: a young couple, 
laughing. The man is him, decades younger.

The woman in the photo is gone now.

He doesn't look at the photo. Doesn't need to.
```

## Pacing Techniques

### Speed Up
- Short sentences
- Rapid dialogue exchanges
- Minimal description
- Quick cuts between locations

### Slow Down
- Longer action blocks
- Silence
- Detailed observation
- Single continuous shot

### Example: Fast Pace
```
BANG. The window SHATTERS.

Sarah dives.

Glass rains down.

SARAH
Go! Go!

Marcus grabs her arm. They run.
```

## Checklist for Screenwriter Style

- [ ] Everything is shown visually, not told
- [ ] Dialogue sounds natural when read aloud
- [ ] Each character has distinct voice
- [ ] Action lines are present tense
- [ ] Emotions conveyed through behavior
- [ ] Scenes have clear purpose/turn
- [ ] Pacing serves the story
- [ ] No camera directions (unless essential)
- [ ] Subtext enriches dialogue
- [ ] White space used effectively

---

## When to Use This Style

- Screenplays (film/TV)
- Stage plays
- Visual storytelling in prose
- Dialogue-heavy fiction
- Dramatic narratives
- Video game scripts
- Podcast scripts
- Comic book scripts

## Common Mistakes

### Overwriting
❌ "She walks slowly across the room with a sad expression on her face, thinking about what happened earlier."

✅ "She walks to the window. Touches the glass. Outside, rain."

### On-the-Nose Dialogue
❌ 
```
JOHN
I'm feeling betrayed because you lied to me.
```

✅
```
JOHN
How long?

MARY
What?

JOHN
How long have you been lying to me?
```

---

## Scene Transitions

- **CUT TO:** (standard, usually implied)
- **FADE TO:** (passage of time)
- **DISSOLVE TO:** (gentle transition)
- **SMASH CUT TO:** (jarring contrast)

Use sparingly. Most transitions are assumed.

---

## Parentheticals (Wrylies)

Use only when action/tone isn't clear:

```
DETECTIVE RIVERA
(into phone)
We found the body.

(to partner)
Call it in.
```

---

**Application Note**: When writing in Screenwriter Laura style, read all dialogue aloud. If it doesn't sound like real speech, rewrite it. Actors need to be able to say these words.
